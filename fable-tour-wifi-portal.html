Archaeological Studies

Documentary research notes on the principal sites of the journey.

Babylon

Babylon, on the Euphrates some ninety kilometres south of Baghdad, ranks among the most consequential cities of the ancient world. First attested in the late third millennium BCE, it rose to imperial prominence under the Amorite king Hammurabi (r. c. 1792–1750 BCE), whose celebrated law stele codified one of humanity’s earliest comprehensive legal systems. The city reached its architectural zenith during the Neo-Babylonian period under Nebuchadnezzar II (r. 605–562 BCE), who rebuilt it on a monumental scale. Archaeological investigation, most systematically by the German excavator Robert Koldewey from 1899, revealed a vast urban plan bisected by the Processional Way and entered through the Ishtar Gate, faced in deep-blue glazed brick bearing relief dragons and bulls sacred to Marduk, the city’s tutelary deity. The reconstructed gate and the remains of the ziggurat Etemenanki—long associated with the biblical Tower of Babel—testify to the ambition of Babylonian engineering. Classical authors counted the legendary Hanging Gardens among the Seven Wonders, though their precise location remains debated among scholars. Babylon was also a centre of learning: its astronomers compiled systematic celestial observations that underpinned later Greek astronomy, while its mathematicians advanced a sophisticated sexagesimal system still echoed in our division of time and the circle. The city’s political fortunes waned after its capture by Cyrus the Great of Persia in 539 BCE, and later under Hellenistic and Parthian rule its population gradually dispersed. Modern conservation has contended with earlier reconstructions and the pressures of the twentieth century, yet the site retains exceptional significance and was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 2019. For the visitor, Babylon offers not merely ruins but a palimpsest of law, religion, science, and empire—the enduring emblem of Mesopotamian civilization and a foundational chapter in the history of urban humanity across the ages.

Borsippa

Borsippa, the modern Birs Nimrud some seventeen kilometres south-west of Babylon, was an ancient Mesopotamian city intimately linked to its greater neighbour. Its history extends into the third millennium BCE, but it flourished especially under the Neo-Babylonian kings, who regarded it as a sister city to Babylon and a centre of considerable religious authority. Borsippa was dedicated to Nabu, the god of wisdom, writing, and scribal craft, and son of Marduk; the annual New Year festival saw Nabu’s statue carried in procession between Borsippa and Babylon, binding the two cities in ritual. The site is dominated by the great ziggurat of the Ezida temple complex, one of the best-preserved structures of its kind in southern Mesopotamia. Rising in receding terraces of mud-brick faced with fired brick, the tower survives to a striking height, its summit partly vitrified—fused into glassy masses by intense heat, whether from lightning or ancient fire—a feature that long fascinated travellers and fed identifications, now discounted, with the Tower of Babel. Nebuchadnezzar II recorded his restoration of the temple in foundation inscriptions, several of which have been recovered, illuminating Neo-Babylonian building practice and royal ideology. Borsippa also housed an important scribal school and library tradition, contributing to the transmission of cuneiform learning in astronomy, omen literature, and lexical texts. Excavations, beginning in the nineteenth century and continued by Austrian missions in recent decades, have clarified the temple plan and urban layout. The towering, scorched silhouette of the ziggurat against the southern plain remains among the most evocative ancient monuments in Iraq. For students of religion and architecture alike, Borsippa preserves the intellectual and devotional world of Babylonia—a place where wisdom itself was worshipped and where the written word held sacred power for many centuries.

Dur-Kurigalzu (Aqar Quf)

Dur-Kurigalzu, today known as Aqar Quf on the western outskirts of Baghdad, was a royal capital founded in the fourteenth century BCE by the Kassite king Kurigalzu I, whose name the city bears—Dur-Kurigalzu meaning “Fortress of Kurigalzu.” The Kassites, a dynasty that ruled Babylonia for some four centuries following the fall of Hammurabi’s line, established this purpose-built administrative and religious centre to consolidate their authority over the region. The site is crowned by its imposing ziggurat, dedicated to Enlil, the chief god of the Mesopotamian pantheon, as part of a temple complex of considerable scale. The surviving core rises dramatically from the plain, its mud-brick construction reinforced at intervals by layers of reed matting and rope—an engineering technique clearly visible in the eroded section and characteristic of Mesopotamian monumental building. For travellers approaching Baghdad, the ziggurat has long served as a landmark, its silhouette mistaken by earlier visitors for a natural hill. Excavations conducted by the Iraqi Directorate of Antiquities in collaboration with foreign scholars during the 1940s revealed not only the temple precinct but also a palace complex, the latter yielding wall paintings, jewellery, and a notable group of objects attesting to the wealth and artistic sophistication of the Kassite court. Among the finds were fragments illustrating the international character of the age, when Babylonia engaged in diplomacy and exchange with Egypt and the Hittites. Dur-Kurigalzu thus offers insight into a frequently overlooked yet formative period of Mesopotamian history, when a foreign dynasty adopted and perpetuated Babylonian religion, language, and scribal tradition. Its ziggurat, partially restored, stands as a sentinel of antiquity at the threshold of the modern capital—a fitting reminder of the continuity of settlement in the Tigris valley across millennia.

Uruk

Uruk, the biblical Erech and modern Warka in southern Iraq, holds a singular place in human history as one of the earliest cities on earth and a crucible of urban civilization. Flourishing from the fourth millennium BCE, Uruk gave its name to an entire archaeological period and witnessed developments of profound and lasting consequence. It was here, around 3300 BCE, that the world’s first writing system emerged—pictographic signs impressed on clay tablets to record administrative transactions, the ancestor of cuneiform script that would serve Mesopotamia for three millennia. The city’s monumental architecture, including the great temple precincts of Eanna, dedicated to the goddess Inanna, and Kullaba, sacred to the sky god Anu, attests to a society capable of mobilising labour on an unprecedented scale and administering complex institutions. German excavations, begun in 1912 and continued for decades, have traced the city’s vast extent, its defensive walls—traditionally attributed to the legendary king Gilgamesh, hero of the eponymous epic—and the evolution of its temples and cylinder-seal art. Uruk pioneered not only writing but also advances in administration, the mass production of pottery, and monumental sculpture, exemplified by the celebrated Warka Vase and the Mask of Warka, among the earliest naturalistic representations of the human face. At its height the city covered several square kilometres and may have housed tens of thousands of inhabitants, making it perhaps the largest settlement of its era. Though gradually abandoned in the early centuries CE as the watercourses shifted, Uruk endures as the archetype of the city itself. To stand among its mounds is to stand at the very dawn of recorded history, where writing, kingship, and urban life were first conceived and set down for all later ages.

Ur

Ur, near modern Nasiriyah in southern Iraq, was among the most powerful and culturally rich cities of ancient Sumer. Occupied from the Ubaid period in the sixth millennium BCE, it rose to its greatest prominence in the late third millennium during the Third Dynasty of Ur (c. 2112–2004 BCE), when kings such as Ur-Nammu and Shulgi presided over a centralised state of remarkable administrative sophistication. The city is dominated by its magnificent ziggurat, built by Ur-Nammu and dedicated to the moon god Nanna (Sin), Ur’s patron deity. Constructed of a mud-brick core encased in fired brick set in bitumen, the monument’s lowest stage has been substantially reconstructed and remains the best-preserved ziggurat in Mesopotamia, its sweeping staircases conveying the grandeur of Sumerian sacred architecture. Ur entered the modern imagination through the excavations of Sir Leonard Woolley between 1922 and 1934, whose discovery of the Royal Cemetery astonished the world. Its richly furnished graves, including that attributed to Queen Puabi, yielded gold vessels, elaborate jewellery, musical instruments such as the lyres adorned with bull’s heads, and the enigmatic Standard of Ur—objects revealing extraordinary craftsmanship and complex funerary practices. Woolley also identified deep deposits of silt that he linked, controversially, to a great flood echoed in Mesopotamian and biblical tradition. Ur was, moreover, a centre of trade reaching to the Gulf and beyond, and a city of scribes, temples, and a thriving economy documented in thousands of administrative tablets. Religious and literary tradition associates Ur with the patriarch Abraham, adding to its resonance across faiths. Today its ziggurat and excavated quarters offer an exceptional window onto Sumerian civilization at its zenith—a city whose art, governance, and religion shaped the foundations of the ancient Near East for centuries to come.

The Mesopotamian Marshes (Al-Ahwar)

The Mesopotamian Marshes, the Ahwar of southern Iraq, form one of the world’s most distinctive wetland landscapes and among the largest inland delta systems in western Asia. Fed by the Tigris and Euphrates as they converge toward the Gulf, these vast expanses of reed beds, lagoons, and shifting waterways have sustained human communities for some five thousand years. The Marsh Arabs, or Ma’dan, developed a remarkable culture adapted wholly to this aquatic environment, dwelling in settlements raised on artificial reed islands and travelling by slender canoes known as mashhuf. Their most striking architectural achievement is the mudhif, a great arched guesthouse woven entirely from reeds—a building tradition whose form is echoed in images on ancient Sumerian cylinder seals, suggesting a continuity of practice spanning millennia. The marshes are often identified in popular tradition with the Garden of Eden and lie close to sites of the earliest Sumerian cities, underscoring the region’s deep antiquity. Ecologically, the wetlands are of global importance, providing habitat for diverse fish, water buffalo, and vast populations of resident and migratory birds along a major flyway. In the late twentieth century the marshes suffered catastrophic drainage, reducing them to a fraction of their former extent and displacing their inhabitants; subsequent re-flooding has allowed a significant, if partial, ecological and cultural recovery. In recognition of their outstanding natural and cultural value, the Ahwar of Southern Iraq were inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 2016, as a mixed property embracing both the marshlands and the nearby archaeological cities of Uruk, Ur, and Eridu. For the traveller, gliding through the still channels among towering reeds offers not merely a scenic experience but an encounter with a living heritage—a way of life that connects the present directly to the dawn of civilization itself.

Hatra (Al-Hadr)

Hatra, in the desert of northern Iraq south-west of Mosul, was a great fortified caravan city that flourished in the first and second centuries CE at the frontier between the Roman and Parthian empires. Strategically positioned on trade routes linking Mesopotamia with the wider Near East, it grew wealthy as a commercial hub and emerged as the capital of a small but resilient Arab kingdom, ruled by princes who later styled themselves kings of the Arabs. Hatra is renowned above all for its formidable circular fortifications—a double wall reinforced by numerous towers and a broad moat—which twice withstood Roman sieges, by the emperors Trajan and Septimius Severus, earning the city a reputation for impregnability. At its heart lay a vast sacred precinct enclosing a series of monumental temples, the grandest dedicated to the sun god Shamash, reflecting a syncretic religious culture that blended Mesopotamian, Arab, and Greco-Roman deities. The architecture of Hatra is its most celebrated feature: great vaulted iwans and richly carved facades fuse Hellenistic and Roman classical elements with Eastern and Parthian forms, while its sculpture—statues of kings, nobles, and divinities in distinctive frontal poses—constitutes a major corpus of Parthian-period art. The city was ultimately captured and destroyed by the Sasanian ruler Shapur I around 240 CE and thereafter abandoned, leaving its monuments to be preserved by the surrounding desert. Rediscovered and studied from the nineteenth century onward, with extensive Iraqi excavations in the twentieth, Hatra was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1985. Despite damage sustained in recent conflict, its towering arches and sculpted columns remain among the most impressive ancient remains in the Middle East—an eloquent monument to a frontier civilization that stood, for a time, between two of antiquity’s greatest powers.
